## Chapter 1: The Four Lenses for AI Opportunities

Finding AI opportunities requires looking at your business from multiple angles. Just as a jeweler examines a gemstone through different lenses to assess its quality, you need to examine your business through different lenses to spot AI possibilities. This chapter introduces four powerful lenses that will help you systematically identify opportunities across your entire operation.

### Why Multiple Lenses Matter

Most business owners have a default perspective when they think about AI. Some immediately think of customer service chatbots. Others imagine automated accounting. Still others envision predictive analytics for sales forecasting. While these are all valid opportunities, focusing on just one area means missing opportunities in others.

The four lenses approach ensures comprehensive coverage:

1. **Customer Experience Lens**: How can AI improve interactions with customers?
2. **Operational Efficiency Lens**: Where can AI streamline internal processes?
3. **Decision-Making Lens**: What decisions could be better with AI assistance?
4. **Growth and Innovation Lens**: How can AI enable new capabilities or offerings?

Each lens reveals different types of opportunities. Used together, they provide a complete picture of where AI can create value in your business.

### Lens 1: The Customer Experience Lens

The customer experience lens focuses on every touchpoint where your business interacts with customers. From first awareness through purchase and ongoing support, this lens helps you identify opportunities to make customer interactions smoother, more personalized, and more effective.

#### What to Look For

When examining your business through the customer experience lens, look for:

**Repetitive Customer Interactions**
- Questions that customers ask repeatedly
- Requests that follow predictable patterns
- Processes that require customers to provide the same information multiple times

**Personalization Opportunities**
- Places where customers receive generic treatment that could be tailored
- Moments where understanding customer preferences would improve outcomes
- Segments of customers with distinct needs that aren't being addressed

**Friction Points**
- Steps in your customer journey that cause frustration or abandonment
- Places where customers wait unnecessarily
- Processes that require customer effort that could be automated

**Communication Gaps**
- Times when customers don't know what's happening with their request
- Channels where response times are slower than customers expect
- Moments when proactive communication would improve the experience

#### Real-World Examples

- Visitors who browsed but didn't buy could receive personalized follow-up emails based on what they viewed

- Customer service inquiries about order status could be handled automatically through an AI assistant

## Chapter 2: Process Mapping for AI

Process mapping is the foundation of opportunity identification. You can't improve what you haven't documented, and you can't automate what you don't understand. This chapter provides practical techniques for mapping your workflows to reveal AI opportunities hidden in the details of how work gets done.

### Why Process Mapping Matters for AI

Most businesses have a general sense of their processes, but the devil is in the details. A process that seems simple at a high level often contains dozens of small decisions, data transformations, and handoffs when examined closely. Each of these details is a potential AI opportunity.

Consider a seemingly simple process like "process customer order":

At a high level: Receive order → Verify payment → Fulfill order → Ship → Confirm

At a detailed level, this might include:
- Reading order details from multiple formats (email, web form, phone call notes)
- Checking inventory across multiple locations
- Verifying payment method and fraud risk
- Determining optimal fulfillment location
- Selecting shipping method based on cost and speed requirements
- Generating pick lists and packing instructions
- Updating inventory systems
- Sending confirmation communications
- Flagging exceptions for human review

Each of these steps might be a candidate for AI assistance or automation.

### The Process Mapping Methodology

Our approach to process mapping for AI opportunity identification involves four levels of detail:

1. **Process Inventory**: What processes exist?
2. **High-Level Flow**: How do processes connect?
3. **Detailed Step Mapping**: What happens at each step?
4. **AI Opportunity Analysis**: Where can AI help?

Let's walk through each level.

#### Level 1: Process Inventory

Start by creating a comprehensive list of all processes in your business. Don't worry about detail yet—just capture what exists.

**Customer-Facing Processes**
- Lead generation and qualification
- Sales process
- Order/purchase processing
- Onboarding
- Ongoing service delivery
- Support and issue resolution
- Billing and payment
- Retention and renewal

**Internal Processes**
- Hiring and onboarding
- Training and development
- Procurement
- Inventory management
- Production/service delivery
- Quality assurance
- Financial management
- Reporting and analysis

**Administrative Processes**
- Document management
- Communication and coordination
- Scheduling and calendar management
- Travel and expense management
- Compliance and regulatory
- IT support

For each process, capture:
- Process name
- Primary owner
- Frequency (how often it runs)
- Approximate time required
- Number of people involved
- Systems used

This inventory alone often reveals opportunities—processes that take longer than they should, involve too many people, or rely on outdated systems.

#### Level 2: High-Level Flow

For your most important processes (prioritize by time consumption, error rate, or strategic importance), create high-level flow diagrams showing:

- Process trigger (what starts it?)
- Major stages
- Decision points
- Handoffs between people or systems
- Process completion

Use simple boxes and arrows. The goal is clarity, not artistic perfection. Tools like Lucidchart, Miro, or even PowerPoint work fine. Paper and sticky notes are perfectly acceptable for initial mapping.

**Example: High-Level Sales Process Flow**

```
[Lead Generated] → [Qualification] → {Qualified?}
                                      ↓
                              [No] → [Nurture]
                                      ↓
                              [Yes] → [Proposal] → [Negotiation] → {Won?}
                                                                       ↓
                                                               [No] → [Lost Analysis]
                                                                       ↓
                                                               [Yes] → [Contract] → [Onboarding]
```

This high-level view helps you see the overall structure and identify major stages for deeper analysis.

#### Level 3: Detailed Step Mapping

Now comes the critical work: mapping each process in detail. For each step in your high-level flow, document:

**What Happens**
- Specific actions taken
- Information processed
- Decisions made
- Outputs created

**Who Does It**
- Role or specific person
- Required skills or knowledge
- Decision authority

**Tools and Systems**
- Software used
- Data sources accessed
- Documents or templates
- Communication channels

**Time and Frequency**
- How long does this step take?
- How often does it occur?
- Is it always the same or variable?

**Pain Points**
- What slows this step down?
- What errors commonly occur?
- What frustrates the person doing it?
- What would make it better?

**Data Elements**
- What information comes in?
- What information goes out?
- Where is data stored?
- What format is it in?

This level of detail is tedious but essential. It's where AI opportunities reveal themselves.

#### Level 4: AI Opportunity Analysis

With detailed process maps in hand, analyze each step for AI potential using these categories:

**Automation Candidates**
- Steps that are purely rule-based
- Data transformation or movement
- Routine communications
- Standard calculations or comparisons

**Augmentation Candidates**
- Steps requiring judgment that could be informed by data
- Decisions that would benefit from pattern recognition
- Reviews that could be pre-screened
- Recommendations that could be generated

**Enhancement Candidates**
- Steps that could be faster with AI assistance
- Outputs that could be higher quality with AI review
- Processes that could handle more variability with AI flexibility
- Experiences that could be more personalized with AI

**Elimination Candidates**
- Steps that exist only because of system limitations
- Workarounds that wouldn't be needed with better AI
- Duplicate efforts that AI could consolidate
- Manual bridges between systems that AI could connect

### The AI Opportunity Patterns

As you map processes, you'll start to see common patterns that indicate AI opportunities:

#### Pattern 1: The Document Processing Bottleneck

**What It Looks Like**: Information arrives in documents (PDFs, emails, images, scans) that someone must read, extract data from, and enter into systems.

**Examples**:
- Invoices received via email that require manual data entry
- Resumes submitted as PDFs that need information extracted
- Forms completed by hand that must be transcribed
- Contracts that need key terms identified

**AI Opportunity**: Document understanding and extraction using OCR, NLP, and structured data extraction.

**Example in Action**: A property management company received 200+ rental applications monthly, each 5-10 pages. Staff spent 30+ minutes per application extracting information. AI-powered document processing reduced this to 2 minutes of review time, with 95% accuracy.

#### Pattern 2: The Classification and Routing Challenge

**What It Looks Like**: Items arrive that must be categorized and directed to the right person or process based on their content.

**Examples**:
- Customer support tickets that need to be routed to the right team
- Job applications that must be matched to open positions
- Expense reports that need approval routing based on amount and type
- Content submissions that require editorial assignment

**AI Opportunity**: Automated classification and intelligent routing based on content analysis.

**Example in Action**: A software company's support team received 1,000+ tickets daily. Manual routing took 4-6 hours and had 15% error rate. AI-powered routing reduced time to minutes with 98% accuracy, getting customers to the right help faster.

#### Pattern 3: The Prediction Dependency

**What It Looks Like**: Decisions are made based on forecasts or estimates that are currently created through simple methods or intuition.

**Examples**:
- Inventory ordering based on last year's sales
- Staff scheduling based on typical patterns
- Project timelines based on similar past projects
- Lead scoring based on simple rules

**AI Opportunity**: Machine learning predictions that incorporate multiple variables and learn from outcomes.

**Example in Action**: A restaurant chain scheduled staff based on day-of-week patterns. AI-powered forecasting that considered weather, local events, holidays, and trends improved labor cost percentage by 3 points while maintaining service levels.

#### Pattern 4: The Personalization Gap

**What It Looks Like**: Customers receive one-size-fits-all treatment where individualized approaches would be more effective.

**Examples**:
- Marketing emails sent to entire lists
- Product recommendations that are generic
- Support responses that don't account for customer history
- Pricing that's the same for all customers

**AI Opportunity**: Personalized recommendations, content, and experiences based on customer data and behavior.

**Example in Action**: An online course provider sent the same promotional emails to all subscribers. AI-powered personalization that considered course history, engagement patterns, and learning goals increased click-through rates by 340% and conversions by 85%.

#### Pattern 5: The Quality Control Bottleneck

**What It Looks Like**: Outputs must be reviewed for quality, compliance, or accuracy, creating a bottleneck.

**Examples**:
- Content that needs editorial review
- Code that requires security review
- Products that need inspection
- Documents that need compliance checking

**AI Opportunity**: AI-powered pre-screening that catches routine issues, allowing human reviewers to focus on complex cases.

**Example in Action**: A marketing agency's editorial review process created a 3-day delay for all content. AI-powered pre-review that checked for brand voice, grammar, and compliance issues allowed human editors to focus on substance and strategy, reducing average review time to 4 hours.

#### Pattern 6: The Data Integration Challenge

**What It Looks Like**: Information exists in multiple systems that don't talk to each other, requiring manual work to connect them.

**Examples**:
- Customer data in CRM that should inform marketing automation
- Inventory data that should trigger purchasing
- Financial data that needs to be consolidated from multiple sources
- Employee data that should flow between HR systems

**AI Opportunity**: Intelligent data integration and synchronization that understands context and relationships.

**Example in Action**: A consulting firm had project data in five different systems. Consultants spent 2 hours weekly updating information across platforms. AI-powered integration that understood project relationships and automatically synchronized relevant data eliminated this busywork entirely.

#### Pattern 7: The Communication Overload

**What It Looks Like**: High volumes of routine communications that consume significant time.

**Examples**:
- Email responses to common questions
- Status updates and check-ins
- Meeting scheduling and coordination
- Routine notifications and reminders

**AI Opportunity**: AI-powered communication assistance that can draft responses, schedule meetings, and manage routine correspondence.

**Example in Action**: A real estate agent received 150+ emails daily from buyers, sellers, and other agents. AI-powered email assistance that drafted responses to common questions and highlighted messages requiring personal attention reduced email time from 4 hours to 90 minutes daily.

### The Process Mapping Workshop

To accelerate your process mapping, conduct a structured workshop with your team:

**Preparation (1 week before)**
- Select 3-5 critical processes to map
- Invite process owners and participants
- Prepare templates and materials
- Schedule 2-3 hour session

**Workshop Agenda**

*Opening (15 minutes)*
- Explain the purpose: finding AI opportunities to make work better
- Set ground rules: no judgment, all ideas welcome, focus on current state (not future)

*Process Selection Review (15 minutes)*
- Confirm the processes to be mapped
- Clarify scope and boundaries
- Identify the process trigger and end point

*High-Level Mapping (30 minutes)*
- Create the major stage flow
- Identify decision points
- Note major handoffs

*Detailed Mapping (60-90 minutes)*
- Break into subgroups for each major stage
- Map detailed steps
- Document pain points and workarounds
- Capture time and frequency data

*Opportunity Identification (30 minutes)*
- Review maps as a group
- Apply AI opportunity patterns
- Flag potential automation and augmentation points
- Prioritize opportunities by impact and feasibility

*Closing (15 minutes)*
- Summarize findings
- Assign next steps for deeper analysis
- Schedule follow-up for implementation planning

### Common Process Mapping Mistakes

Avoid these pitfalls when mapping processes:

**Mapping the Ideal, Not the Reality**
People often describe how a process should work, not how it actually works. Watch for phrases like "typically" or "usually." Ask: "What happens when things don't go smoothly?" The exceptions often reveal the best opportunities.

**Staying Too High-Level**
If your process map looks like a strategy diagram, you've stayed too high. You need to see individual actions, data elements, and decision criteria. When in doubt, go deeper.

**Ignoring Shadow Processes**
The official process map may not match reality. People develop workarounds, shadow systems, and informal procedures to get work done. These are goldmines for AI opportunities because they indicate where current systems are failing.

**Forgetting the Human Experience**
Process mapping isn't just about steps—it's about the people performing them. What frustrates them? What takes longer than it should? What feels like a waste of their skills? These human factors often point to the highest-value opportunities.

**Not Following the Data**
Every process transforms data. Map what data comes in, what happens to it, and what goes out. Data flows reveal integration opportunities and transformation needs that process steps alone might miss.

### From Mapping to Action

Process mapping is only valuable if it leads to action. As you complete your mapping:

1. **Validate with Reality**: Walk through the process yourself or observe it being performed. Does your map match reality?

2. **Quantify the Pain**: For each opportunity, estimate the current cost in time, money, or frustration. This helps with prioritization.

3. **Identify Quick Wins**: Look for opportunities that are high-impact and low-complexity. These build momentum for larger initiatives.

4. **Connect to Strategy**: Ensure opportunities align with business priorities. An opportunity that doesn't support your strategy isn't worth pursuing, no matter how interesting.

5. **Build the Business Case**: For prioritized opportunities, develop rough estimates of implementation cost and expected return. This enables informed decision-making.

### Chapter Summary

Process mapping reveals AI opportunities hidden in the details of how work gets done:

1. **Create a process inventory** of all business processes
2. **Map high-level flows** to understand overall structure
3. **Document detailed steps** including actions, data, decisions, and pain points
4. **Apply AI opportunity patterns** to identify automation and augmentation candidates
5. **Create an opportunity register** to track and prioritize findings

Common patterns to watch for include document processing bottlenecks, classification challenges, prediction dependencies, personalization gaps, quality control bottlenecks, data integration challenges, and communication overload.

### Assessing Data Quality

Volume alone doesn't make data valuable. Quality matters significantly for AI success.

#### The Dimensions of Data Quality

**Accuracy**: Is the data correct?
- Are there errors in the data?
- How is accuracy verified?
- What's the error rate?

**Completeness**: Is all expected data present?
- What percentage of records have all fields?
- Are there systematic patterns in missing data?
- Can missing data be inferred or filled?

**Consistency**: Is the data uniform?
- Are formats consistent (dates, addresses, names)?
- Are categories standardized?
- Is the same information recorded the same way across systems?

**Timeliness**: Is the data current?
- How often is it updated?
- Is there a lag between events and recording?
- How quickly does the data become stale?

**Relevance**: Is the data useful for the intended purpose?
- Does it capture the information needed?
- Is it at the right level of granularity?
- Does it relate to the outcomes you're trying to predict?

# Part III: Evaluation and Prioritization

## Chapter 7: The Impact Scoring System

Not all AI opportunities are created equal. Some deliver transformative value; others provide marginal improvements. This chapter introduces a comprehensive impact scoring system to quantify the potential value of each opportunity, enabling objective comparison and informed prioritization.

### Why Impact Scoring Matters

Small businesses have limited resources—time, money, and attention. You can't pursue every AI opportunity simultaneously. Impact scoring helps you:

- Compare apples-to-apples across different types of opportunities
- Focus resources on highest-value initiatives
- Build the business case for investment
- Set realistic expectations for outcomes
- Measure actual results against predictions

Without systematic scoring, decisions become political or haphazard. With it, you can make rational, defensible choices about where to invest.

### The Four Dimensions of Impact

We evaluate impact across four dimensions:

1. **Time Savings**: How much human time will this free up?
2. **Quality Improvements**: How much will error rates, consistency, or outcomes improve?
3. **Revenue Impact**: How will this affect top-line growth?
4. **Strategic Value**: How does this contribute to long-term competitive position?

Each dimension is scored 1-5, then weighted based on business priorities to create an overall impact score.

### Dimension 1: Quantifying Time Savings

Time savings is often the easiest impact to calculate and the most immediate to realize.

#### Calculating Time Savings

**Step 1: Identify Affected Roles**
Who will be impacted by this AI opportunity?
- Specific individuals or roles
- Number of people affected
- Their hourly cost (salary + benefits)

**Step 2: Measure Current Time Spent**
How much time is currently spent on the activities to be automated or augmented?
- Time per occurrence
- Frequency (daily, weekly, monthly)
- Annual time total

**Step 3: Estimate Time Reduction**
What percentage of time will be saved?
- Fully automated: 90-100% time savings
- Heavily augmented: 50-75% time savings
- Moderately assisted: 20-40% time savings
- Slightly improved: 5-15% time savings

**Step 4: Calculate Annual Value**
```
Annual Hours Saved = Current Hours × Time Reduction %
Annual Value = Annual Hours Saved × Hourly Cost
```

#### Time Savings Scoring Rubric

| Score | Annual Hours Saved | Annual Value |
|-------|-------------------|--------------|
| 1 | < 100 hours | < $5,000 |
| 2 | 100-500 hours | $5,000-$25,000 |
| 3 | 500-2,000 hours | $25,000-$100,000 |
| 4 | 2,000-5,000 hours | $100,000-$250,000 |
| 5 | > 5,000 hours | > $250,000 |

#### Time Savings Example

*Opportunity*: Automate invoice processing

**Current State**:
- 3 AP staff spending 10 hours/week each on invoice processing
- Hourly cost: $30/hour
- Current annual time: 1,560 hours
- Current annual cost: $46,800

**With AI**:
- 80% of invoices processed automatically
- Remaining 20% require 50% less time (AI-assisted)
- Total time reduction: 90%
- Annual hours saved: 1,404 hours
- Annual value: $42,120

**Score**: 3 (500-2,000 hours, $25,000-$100,000)

## Chapter 8: Feasibility Assessment

High impact means little if an opportunity isn't feasible to implement. This chapter guides you through assessing the technical, data, organizational, and resource feasibility of AI opportunities—ensuring you pursue opportunities you can actually achieve.

### The Four Dimensions of Feasibility

We assess feasibility across four dimensions:

1. **Technical Complexity**: How difficult is the technical implementation?
2. **Data Readiness**: Do you have the data needed, and what's its quality?
3. **Change Management**: How difficult will organizational adoption be?
4. **Resource Requirements**: What investment is required in time, money, and expertise?

Each dimension is scored 1-5 (lower is better for feasibility), then combined to create an overall feasibility score.

### Dimension 1: Technical Complexity Evaluation

Technical complexity assesses how difficult the AI solution will be to build, integrate, and maintain.

#### Technical Complexity Factors

**AI Maturity of the Solution**
- Well-established, off-the-shelf solutions (low complexity)
- Proven approaches requiring customization (medium)
- Emerging techniques with limited track record (high)
- Cutting-edge research applications (very high)

**Integration Requirements**
- Standalone application with minimal integration (low)
- Integration with one or two existing systems (medium)
- Complex integration with multiple core systems (high)
- Real-time integration with critical systems (very high)

**Customization Needs**
- Out-of-the-box solution fits needs (low)
- Configuration and tuning required (medium)
- Significant customization needed (high)
- Custom model development required (very high)

**Scalability and Performance**
- Handles current and expected volume easily (low)
- Requires optimization for scale (medium)
- Significant infrastructure investment needed (high)
- Uncertain performance at scale (very high)

#### Technical Complexity Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Simple, off-the-shelf implementation | Chatbot using standard platform, basic automation |
| 2 | Moderate customization of proven solutions | Custom-trained classification, API integrations |
| 3 | Significant customization, multiple integrations | Complex workflow automation, custom models |
| 4 | Complex custom development, novel applications | Sophisticated prediction systems, real-time AI |
| 5 | Research-grade, unproven approaches | Cutting-edge generative AI, novel architectures |

#### Technical Complexity Assessment Questions

- Is there an off-the-shelf solution that meets 80% of needs?
- How many systems need to be integrated?
- What's the criticality of system uptime?
- Do you have technical expertise in-house?
- What's the vendor ecosystem maturity?
- How much customization is truly required?

### Dimension 2: Data Readiness Scoring

Data readiness assesses whether you have the data needed, at the quality required, accessible in the right way.

#### Data Readiness Factors

**Data Availability**
- All required data exists and is accessible (ready)
- Most data exists, some gaps to fill (nearly ready)
- Significant data collection needed (requires work)
- Major data gaps, uncertain availability (challenging)
- Data doesn't exist and can't be obtained (blocked)

**Data Quality**
- High quality, well-structured, clean (ready)
- Good quality with minor issues (nearly ready)
- Moderate quality, requires cleaning (requires work)
- Poor quality, significant issues (challenging)
- Unusable quality (blocked)

**Data Volume**
- Sufficient historical data for training (ready)
- Adequate data, may need augmentation (nearly ready)
- Limited data, creative approaches needed (requires work)
- Insufficient data, collection time required (challenging)
- No meaningful data volume (blocked)

**Data Accessibility**
- Easily accessible via APIs or direct access (ready)
- Accessible with minor integration work (nearly ready)
- Requires data engineering effort (requires work)
- Complex extraction and transformation needed (challenging)
- Data locked in inaccessible systems (blocked)

#### Data Readiness Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Data is available, high quality, sufficient volume, easily accessible |
| 2 | Minor gaps in availability, quality, or access; easily addressable |
| 3 | Moderate data work required; collection, cleaning, or integration needed |
| 4 | Significant data challenges; major collection or quality improvement needed |
| 5 | Critical data gaps that are difficult or impossible to address |

#### Data Readiness Assessment Questions

- What specific data does the AI need?
- Do you currently collect this data?
- What's the quality of existing data?
- How much historical data do you have?
- How is the data stored and accessed?
- Are there privacy or compliance constraints?
- How long would it take to fill data gaps?

### Dimension 3: Change Management Difficulty

Change management assesses how difficult it will be to get people to adopt and effectively use the AI solution.

#### Change Management Factors

**User Impact**
- Affects small number of users in limited way (easy)
- Affects multiple users with workflow changes (moderate)
- Significantly changes daily work for many (difficult)
- Transforms roles and responsibilities (very difficult)
- Threatens jobs or core identity (extremely difficult)

**Skill Requirements**
- No new skills required (easy)
- Minor skill development needed (moderate)
- Significant training required (difficult)
- New expertise or roles needed (very difficult)
- Fundamental capability gaps (extremely difficult)

**Cultural Fit**
- Aligns well with existing culture (easy)
- Neutral, no cultural conflict (moderate)
- Requires some cultural adjustment (difficult)
- Significant cultural change needed (very difficult)
- Conflicts with core values (extremely difficult)

**Stakeholder Complexity**
- Single team, clear owner (easy)
- Multiple teams, aligned interests (moderate)
- Cross-functional with competing priorities (difficult)
- External stakeholders involved (very difficult)
- High-stakes, visible, controversial (extremely difficult)

#### Change Management Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Minimal change impact, enthusiastic users, clear ownership |
| 2 | Moderate impact, users see benefit, straightforward rollout |
| 3 | Significant workflow changes, training needed, some resistance |
| 4 | Major transformation, skill gaps, potential resistance, complex stakeholders |
| 5 | Fundamental change, job impacts, strong resistance, high visibility |

#### Change Management Assessment Questions

- Who will use this AI solution?
- How will their work change?
- Do they see the benefit, or does it feel imposed?
- What training will be required?
- Who could resist, and why?
- What's the history of technology adoption in your organization?
- How visible and high-stakes is this initiative?

### Dimension 4: Resource Requirements

Resource requirements assess the investment needed in time, money, and expertise.

#### Resource Requirement Factors

**Financial Investment**
- <$10K total investment (minimal)
- $10K-$50K (modest)
- $50K-$200K (significant)
- $200K-$500K (major)
- >$500K (substantial)

**Time to Value**
- < 1 month to initial results (immediate)
- 1-3 months (quick)
- 3-6 months (moderate)
- 6-12 months (extended)
- > 12 months (long-term)

**Expertise Requirements**
- Can be implemented with existing team (internal)
- Requires limited external support (minimal external)
- Needs specialized expertise for parts (some external)
- Requires significant external expertise (heavy external)
- Demands specialized team or vendor (fully external)

**Ongoing Commitment**
- Minimal ongoing effort (low maintenance)
- Some monitoring and tuning (moderate maintenance)
- Regular attention and updates required (significant maintenance)
- Dedicated resources needed (high maintenance)
- Continuous significant investment (major maintenance)

#### Resource Scoring Rubric

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | <$10K, <1 month, internal expertise, low maintenance |
| 2 | $10K-$50K, 1-3 months, minimal external, moderate maintenance |
| 3 | $50K-$200K, 3-6 months, some external, significant maintenance |
| 4 | $200K-$500K, 6-12 months, heavy external, high maintenance |
| 5 | >$500K, >12 months, fully external, major ongoing commitment |

#### Resource Assessment Questions

- What's your budget for AI initiatives?
- How quickly do you need to see results?
- What expertise do you have in-house?
- What's your appetite for external vendors?
- What ongoing resources can you commit?
- What's the opportunity cost of this investment?

### Calculating Overall Feasibility Score

Combine the four feasibility dimensions:

**Calculation**:
```
Overall Feasibility Score = 
  (Technical Score + Data Score + Change Score + Resource Score) / 4
```

**Interpretation**:
- 1.0-2.0: Highly feasible, proceed with confidence
- 2.1-3.0: Moderately feasible, address specific concerns
- 3.1-4.0: Challenging, requires significant mitigation
- 4.1-5.0: High risk, reconsider or break into smaller pieces

**Example Calculation**:
| Dimension | Score |
|-----------|-------|
| Technical Complexity | 2 |
| Data Readiness | 3 |
| Change Management | 2 |
| Resource Requirements | 2 |
| **Overall** | **2.25** |

This opportunity is moderately feasible—proceed with attention to data readiness.

### Feasibility Mitigation Strategies

When feasibility scores are high, consider these mitigation approaches:

**Technical Complexity Mitigation**
- Start with proven, off-the-shelf solutions
- Use pilot projects to reduce uncertainty
- Engage experienced implementation partners
- Break complex solutions into phases

**Data Readiness Mitigation**
- Launch data collection immediately
- Use data augmentation techniques
- Consider synthetic data generation
- Start with opportunities requiring less data

**Change Management Mitigation**
- Involve users early in design
- Communicate benefits clearly
- Provide comprehensive training
- Identify and engage champions
- Address concerns transparently

**Resource Mitigation**
- Phase implementation to spread costs
- Use SaaS solutions to reduce upfront investment
- Build internal capabilities gradually
- Prioritize quick wins to fund larger initiatives

### Chapter Summary

Feasibility assessment ensures you pursue achievable opportunities:

1. **Technical Complexity** evaluates implementation difficulty
2. **Data Readiness** assesses data availability, quality, and accessibility
3. **Change Management** gauges organizational adoption difficulty
4. **Resource Requirements** measures investment needs

Lower scores indicate higher feasibility. Use mitigation strategies to address feasibility challenges, and consider breaking high-feasibility-score opportunities into smaller, more achievable pieces.

## Chapter 9: Building Your Opportunity Portfolio

With impact and feasibility scores in hand, you're ready to build a strategic portfolio of AI opportunities. This chapter shows you how to balance different types of initiatives, manage dependencies, and sequence your AI journey for maximum success.

### The Portfolio Approach

Successful AI adoption isn't about picking one opportunity—it's about building a portfolio that balances:

- **Quick wins vs. long-term bets**: Immediate value vs. transformational potential
- **Risk levels**: Safe, proven approaches vs. innovative experiments
- **Resource requirements**: Low-investment starters vs. major initiatives
- **Capability building**: What you can do now vs. what you're building toward

A well-constructed portfolio delivers value early while building toward bigger future gains.

### The Impact/Feasibility Matrix

Plot your opportunities on a 2x2 matrix using impact (y-axis) and feasibility (x-axis, inverted so right side is better):

```
High Impact │  Strategic    │  Priority
            │  Bets         │  Projects
            │               │
            ├───────────────┼───────────────┤
            │  Avoid        │  Quick
Low Impact  │  (Why?)       │  Wins
            │               │
            └───────────────┴───────────────┘
              Low Feasibility   High Feasibility
```

**Quadrant Definitions**:

**Quick Wins (High Impact, High Feasibility)**
- Immediate priorities
- Implement first
- Build momentum and credibility
- Fund larger initiatives

**Priority Projects (High Impact, Lower Feasibility)**
- Major strategic initiatives
- Plan carefully
- Address feasibility challenges
- May require phasing

**Strategic Bets (Lower Impact, Lower Feasibility)**
- Experimental initiatives
- Small bets with potential
- Learn and iterate
- Kill quickly if not working

**Avoid (Lower Impact, High Feasibility)**
- Why bother?
- Only if supporting other initiatives
- Lowest priority

### Balancing Your Portfolio

A healthy portfolio contains opportunities from each category:

#### Quick Wins (30-40% of portfolio)

**Characteristics**:
- High impact, high feasibility
- Can implement within 1-3 months
- Deliver visible value quickly
- Build organizational confidence

**Examples**:
- Customer service chatbot for FAQs
- Email automation for common responses
- Basic document processing automation
- Simple personalization

**Purpose**: Generate immediate returns, build momentum, fund future initiatives

#### Priority Projects (40-50% of portfolio)

**Characteristics**:
- High impact, moderate feasibility challenges
- Require 3-12 months to implement
- Significant strategic value
- Need careful planning and resources

**Examples**:
- Predictive analytics for key decisions
- Comprehensive process automation
- Advanced personalization
- AI-powered product features

**Purpose**: Deliver major value, build capabilities, create competitive advantage

#### Strategic Bets (10-20% of portfolio)

**Characteristics**:
- Uncertain impact or feasibility
- Experimental in nature
- Small initial investment
- Potential for breakthrough

**Examples**:
- Emerging AI technology pilots
- New business model experiments
- Novel application of AI to your domain
- Exploratory data science projects

**Purpose**: Learn, discover unexpected opportunities, position for future trends

### Dependency Management

AI opportunities often depend on each other. Understanding these dependencies is crucial for sequencing.

#### Types of Dependencies

**Data Dependencies**
- Opportunity B requires data collected for Opportunity A
- Shared data infrastructure needed
- Data quality improvements benefit multiple opportunities

**Technical Dependencies**
- Opportunity B builds on platform built for Opportunity A
- Shared integration points
- Common infrastructure requirements

**Capability Dependencies**
- Opportunity B requires skills developed implementing Opportunity A
- Organizational learning from early projects
- Change management foundation

**Resource Dependencies**
- Opportunity B funded by returns from Opportunity A
- Shared team or vendor resources
- Sequential resource requirements

#### Mapping Dependencies

Create a dependency map for your opportunities:

```
[Data Collection] → [Basic Analytics] → [Predictive Models]
       ↓                                    ↓
[Process Automation] ←────────── [Decision Support]
```

This map shows that:
- Data collection must come first
- Basic analytics can proceed in parallel with process automation
- Predictive models depend on basic analytics
- Decision support depends on both predictive models and process automation

### Sequencing for Success

Based on your impact/feasibility matrix and dependency map, sequence your opportunities:

#### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-3)

**Focus**: Quick wins that build foundation

**Typical Initiatives**:
- Data collection and quality improvements
- Simple automation of high-volume tasks
- Basic chatbot or self-service
- Initial analytics and dashboards

**Success Criteria**:
- Measurable time savings
- User adoption and satisfaction
- Data infrastructure in place
- Organizational confidence built

#### Phase 2: Expansion (Months 4-9)

**Focus**: Priority projects that leverage foundation

**Typical Initiatives**:
- More sophisticated automation
- Predictive capabilities
- Advanced personalization
- Integrated workflows

**Success Criteria**:
- Significant efficiency gains
- Revenue impact visible
- Capabilities developed
- ROI demonstrated

#### Phase 3: Transformation (Months 10-18)

**Focus**: Strategic initiatives that create competitive advantage

**Typical Initiatives**:
- AI-powered products or services
- Predictive decision-making
- New business models
- Platform capabilities

**Success Criteria**:
- Competitive differentiation
- New revenue streams
- Market position improved
- Sustainable advantage created

### Risk Diversification

Don't put all your eggs in one basket. Diversify your portfolio across:

**Technology Risk**
- Mix proven technologies with emerging ones
- Don't bet everything on unproven approaches
- Have fallback options

**Vendor Risk**
- Avoid over-dependence on single vendors
- Consider open-source alternatives
- Maintain internal capabilities

**Implementation Risk**
- Don't have all projects at the same stage
- Mix easy and challenging implementations
- Stagger start dates

**Business Risk**
- Spread across customer-facing and internal opportunities
- Balance cost reduction and revenue growth
- Include both defensive and offensive plays

### Building the Business Case

For each opportunity in your portfolio, develop a business case:

#### Business Case Components

**Problem/Opportunity Statement**
- What are we trying to solve or achieve?
- Why does it matter?
- What happens if we do nothing?

**Proposed Solution**
- What AI approach will we use?
- How will it work?
- What are the alternatives?

**Impact Analysis**
- Quantified benefits (using scoring from Chapter 7)
- Strategic value
- Risk reduction
- Capability building

**Feasibility Assessment**
- Technical approach
- Data requirements
- Resource needs
- Risk factors

**Implementation Plan**
- Timeline and milestones
- Resource requirements
- Success metrics
- Governance approach

**Financial Analysis**
- Investment required
- Expected returns
- Payback period
- Sensitivity analysis

### Portfolio Governance

Establish ongoing governance for your AI portfolio:

#### Portfolio Review Cadence

**Monthly**: Quick win progress and blockers
**Quarterly**: Full portfolio review, priority adjustments
**Annually**: Strategic reassessment, long-term planning

#### Review Questions

- Are we on track with quick wins?
- Are priority projects progressing?
- Are strategic bets showing promise?
- Do we need to adjust sequencing?
- Are we learning and adapting?
- Should any initiatives be killed or paused?

#### Decision Framework

Use these criteria for portfolio decisions:

**Continue**: Meeting milestones, showing promise
**Accelerate**: Exceeding expectations, high potential
**Pivot**: Challenges encountered, approach needs adjustment
**Pause**: Blocked by dependencies or resources
**Kill**: Not delivering, better opportunities exist

### Communicating Your Portfolio

Share your AI opportunity portfolio with stakeholders:

**Executive Summary**
- Portfolio overview and balance
- Expected value and timeline
- Resource requirements
- Key risks and mitigations

**Detailed Roadmap**
- Sequenced initiatives
- Dependencies and critical path
- Milestones and decision points
- Success metrics

**Progress Dashboard**
- Initiative status
- Value delivered to date
- Learnings and adjustments
- Upcoming priorities

### Chapter Summary

Building your opportunity portfolio brings together all the assessment work:

1. **Use the impact/feasibility matrix** to categorize opportunities
2. **Balance quick wins, priority projects, and strategic bets**
3. **Map and manage dependencies** to enable effective sequencing
4. **Phase implementation** from foundation to transformation
5. **Diversify risk** across technologies, vendors, and business areas
6. **Develop business cases** for prioritized opportunities
7. **Establish governance** for ongoing portfolio management

Your portfolio is a living document—review regularly, adapt based on learnings, and maintain balance as you progress on your AI journey.
