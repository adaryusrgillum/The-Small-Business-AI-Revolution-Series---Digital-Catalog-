# Introduction: The Power of Focused AI Implementation

## Why 90 Days?

In the fast-paced world of small business, time is your most precious resource. You can't afford to spend six months implementing an AI solution that might not deliver results. You need something faster, more focused, and more certain. That's where the 90-day sprint comes in.

The 90-day timeframe isn't arbitrary. It's long enough to achieve meaningful results but short enough to maintain focus and momentum. It's the sweet spot between "quick wins" that don't transform your business and massive projects that never seem to end.

Think about what you could accomplish in 90 days with complete focus:
- Transform your customer service with an AI chatbot that actually works
- Automate your invoice processing and cut administrative time by 70%
- Deploy an AI-powered marketing system that generates qualified leads while you sleep
- Build a predictive inventory system that prevents stockouts and overstock

These aren't pipe dreams. These are real results that small businesses achieve every day through focused AI sprints.

## The Small Business Advantage

Here's something the big consultancies won't tell you: small businesses have a massive advantage when it comes to AI implementation. While enterprises are bogged down in committees, approval processes, and change management programs that span years, you can move fast.

You can make decisions in a day that would take a corporation a month. You can pivot when something isn't working without navigating layers of bureaucracy. You can get your entire team aligned in a single meeting, not a series of workshops spread across quarters.

The 90-day sprint framework is designed to leverage these advantages. It's built for speed, flexibility, and results.

## The Sprint Philosophy

Before we dive into the details, let's establish the core principles that make the 90-day sprint work:

### 1. Focus Beats Breadth
You'll accomplish more by doing one thing exceptionally well than by doing five things poorly. Each sprint targets a single, well-defined outcome.

### 2. Done Is Better Than Perfect
A working solution deployed in 90 days beats a perfect solution that's still in development six months later. We iterate and improve, but we ship.

### 3. People Before Technology
The best AI tool in the world is useless if your team doesn't adopt it. Every sprint includes equal attention to technical implementation and human adoption.

### 4. Measure Everything
If you can't measure it, you can't improve it. Every sprint has clear metrics from day one, and we track them religiously.

### 5. Learn and Adapt
No plan survives contact with reality. The sprint framework includes regular checkpoints to assess progress and adjust course as needed.

## A Preview of Your 90-Day Journey

Here's what the next three months will look like:

**Days -14 to 0: Preparation**
You'll define your sprint objective, assemble your team, and prepare your resources. This foundation work is crucial—skipping it is like building a house without a blueprint.

**Days 1-30: Foundation**
Month one is about getting the basics right. You'll select and set up your tools, train your team, and deploy core functionality. By the end of month one, you'll have something working.

**Days 31-60: Integration**
Month two focuses on making the AI a seamless part of your operations. You'll redesign workflows, train users, and optimize performance. By the end of month two, your team will be using the AI daily.

**Days 61-90: Validation and Scale**
Month three is about proving value and preparing for the future. You'll measure results, document everything, and plan your next sprint. By day 90, you'll have a working AI solution and a clear path forward.

**Post-Sprint: Continuous Improvement**
The sprint ends, but the journey continues. You'll conduct a retrospective, capture lessons learned, and use your momentum to launch your next sprint.

# Part I: Sprint Preparation (Days -14 to 0)

The two weeks before your sprint officially begins are arguably the most important. This is when you set the foundation for everything that follows. Rush this phase, and you'll pay for it later with confusion, delays, and missed objectives. Do it right, and your sprint will flow smoothly from one milestone to the next.

## Chapter 1: Sprint Planning and Setup

### Defining Sprint Objectives

Every successful sprint starts with a clear, compelling objective. Not a vague aspiration like "improve customer service with AI" but a specific, measurable outcome like "reduce average customer service response time from 4 hours to 30 minutes by deploying an AI chatbot that handles 70% of common inquiries without human intervention."

The difference matters. Vague objectives lead to scope creep, endless debates about priorities, and ultimately, projects that never quite finish. Specific objectives create clarity, enable decisive action, and provide a clear finish line.

#### The SMART-Sprint Framework

For AI sprints, we use a variation of SMART goals tailored to the unique challenges of AI implementation:

**Specific:** What exactly will the AI do? Which process will it improve? For which users?

**Measurable:** How will you know it's working? What metrics will you track?

**Achievable:** Can this realistically be done in 90 days with your resources?

**Relevant:** Does this align with your broader business objectives?

**Time-bound:** What will be delivered by day 30, day 60, and day 90?

**Sprint-appropriate:** Is the scope right for a sprint? (Not too big, not too small)

Let's look at some examples:

| Weak Objective | Strong Sprint Objective |
|---------------|------------------------|
| "Use AI for marketing" | "Deploy an AI email personalization system that increases open rates by 25% and click-through rates by 15% for our weekly newsletter by day 90" |
| "Improve inventory management" | "Implement AI demand forecasting for our top 50 SKUs, reducing stockouts by 40% and overstock by 30% within 90 days" |
| "Automate customer support" | "Launch an AI chatbot that resolves 60% of tier-1 support tickets without human intervention, reducing average response time from 6 hours to 15 minutes" |

#### The Objective Definition Workshop

Gather your key stakeholders for a two-hour workshop to define your sprint objective. Here's the agenda:

**Opening (15 minutes):** Review the sprint concept and ground rules. No devices, full participation.

**Problem Identification (30 minutes):** What business problem are we trying to solve? Be specific. "We're losing customers because of slow support response times" is better than "customer service needs improvement."

**Solution Brainstorming (30 minutes):** How might AI solve this problem? Don't evaluate yet—just generate possibilities.

**Feasibility Filtering (30 minutes):** Which solutions could realistically be implemented in 90 days? Cross-reference against the criteria we'll discuss in Chapter 2.

**Objective Refinement (15 minutes):** Take the most promising solution and craft it into a SMART-Sprint objective.

Document everything. The ideas you don't use now might become your next sprint.

### Assembling Your Sprint Team

AI sprints don't succeed because of technology—they succeed because of people. Your sprint team needs the right mix of skills, authority, and commitment.

#### The Core Sprint Team

**Sprint Leader (1 person)**
This is the single person accountable for the sprint's success. They coordinate the team, remove blockers, and make day-to-day decisions. In a small business, this might be the owner, an operations manager, or a department head. The key requirements are:
- Authority to make decisions about resources and priorities
- Availability to dedicate significant time to the sprint (at least 10 hours per week)
- Understanding of both the business problem and the technical possibilities

**Technical Lead (1 person)**
This person handles the technical implementation. They might be:
- An in-house IT person or developer
- A technically-savvy team member willing to learn
- An external consultant or contractor (more on this below)

The technical lead doesn't need to be an AI expert—that's what the tools and training are for—but they need to be comfortable with technology and willing to figure things out.

**Domain Expert (1-2 people)**
These are the people who understand the business process you're improving. If you're automating customer service, you need someone who knows customer service inside and out. If you're optimizing inventory, you need someone who lives and breathes your supply chain.

Domain experts provide crucial input on requirements, test the solution, and help with user training. They don't need technical skills, but they need to be available and engaged.

**End Users (2-4 people)**
These are the people who will actually use the AI solution day-to-day. Including them from the start ensures the solution meets real needs and builds buy-in for adoption.

#### Extended Team Members

Depending on your sprint, you might also need:

**Executive Sponsor:** Someone with authority to resolve conflicts, approve budget overruns, and remove organizational barriers. In a small business, this is often the owner.

**External Consultant:** If you don't have internal technical capability, consider hiring a consultant for the technical lead role. Look for someone with experience in AI implementation for small businesses, not just technical expertise.

**Vendor Support:** Your AI tool vendors can be valuable resources. Establish relationships with their support and success teams before the sprint begins.

#### Team Commitment Agreement

Before the sprint starts, have every team member sign a commitment agreement. This isn't a legal document—it's a psychological one. It should include:

- Time commitment (hours per week during the sprint)
- Availability for key meetings (list them specifically)
- Authority to make decisions in their area
- Commitment to the sprint objective

Here's a template:

```
SPRINT TEAM COMMITMENT AGREEMENT

Sprint: [Sprint Name]
Dates: [Start Date] to [End Date]

I, [Name], commit to:

□ Dedicating [X] hours per week to this sprint
□ Attending all scheduled sprint meetings (list attached)
□ Responding to sprint communications within 24 hours
□ Making decisions in my area of authority without unnecessary delay
□ Supporting the sprint objective even when it requires change or extra effort
□ Escalating blockers immediately when they occur

I understand that this sprint is a priority for our business and that my contribution is essential to its success.

Signed: _________________ Date: _______
```

### Resource Preparation

A sprint without resources is just a wish. Before day 1, you need to secure:

#### Budget

Be realistic about costs. A typical AI sprint budget includes:

| Category | Typical Range | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| AI Tools/Software | $500-$5,000 | Depends on tools and scale |
| External Help | $2,000-$15,000 | If needed for technical implementation |
| Training | $500-$2,000 | Courses, books, certifications |
| Contingency (20%) | Variable | For unexpected expenses |

Don't let limited budget stop you. Many powerful AI tools have free tiers or affordable small business plans. The most important investment is time, not money.

#### Time

Calculate the total time investment:

- Sprint Leader: 10-15 hours/week × 12 weeks = 120-180 hours
- Technical Lead: 15-25 hours/week × 12 weeks = 180-300 hours
- Domain Experts: 5-10 hours/week × 12 weeks = 60-120 hours each
- End Users: 3-5 hours/week during training and rollout = 20-40 hours each

Make sure everyone understands and commits to this time investment upfront.

#### Tools and Infrastructure

Before day 1, ensure you have:

- **Hardware:** Adequate computers for the sprint team (most AI tools are cloud-based, so requirements are usually minimal)
- **Software:** Licenses and accounts for all necessary tools
- **Data Access:** The sprint team needs access to the data the AI will use
- **Test Environment:** A safe place to experiment without affecting live operations
- **Documentation Space:** Where you'll store sprint documentation (shared drive, wiki, project management tool)

#### Data Preparation

AI needs data. Before your sprint begins:

1. **Identify data sources:** Where is the data the AI will need? Is it accessible?
2. **Assess data quality:** Is the data clean, complete, and accurate? If not, budget time for cleaning.
3. **Ensure data access:** Can the sprint team access the data they need? Are there privacy or security considerations?
4. **Prepare sample data:** Have sample datasets ready for testing and development.

### Stakeholder Alignment

Your sprint team isn't the only group that matters. You need alignment from everyone who will be affected by or can influence the sprint's success.

#### Stakeholder Mapping

Identify your stakeholders and their interests:

| Stakeholder | Interest | Influence | Engagement Strategy |
|-------------|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Business Owner | ROI, business impact | High | Regular updates, final approval on changes |
| Department Heads | Impact on their teams | Medium-High | Early involvement, input on requirements |
| End Users | Job impact, usability | Medium | Participation in design, comprehensive training |
| IT/Security | Technical compliance | Medium | Early review, ongoing consultation |
| Customers | Service quality | Low-Medium | Communication about changes, feedback channels |

#### The Alignment Meeting

Hold a stakeholder alignment meeting before the sprint begins. Agenda:

1. **Sprint Overview (15 min):** What we're doing, why, and how
2. **Objective Presentation (15 min):** The specific goal and how we'll measure success
3. **Impact Discussion (30 min):** How this will affect each stakeholder group
4. **Concern Resolution (30 min):** Address questions and concerns
5. **Communication Plan (15 min):** How we'll keep stakeholders informed
6. **Commitment Requests (15 min):** What we need from each stakeholder

Document agreements and follow up with written confirmation.

#### Communication Plan

Establish how you'll communicate with stakeholders throughout the sprint:

- **Weekly Updates:** Brief email or message with progress highlights
- **Bi-weekly Demos:** Show working functionality to maintain engagement
- **Milestone Reviews:** Formal presentations at days 30, 60, and 90
- **Escalation Path:** Clear process for raising and resolving issues

### Pre-Sprint Checklist

Before day 1, verify:

- [ ] Sprint objective defined and documented
- [ ] Success metrics established and baselined
- [ ] Core team assembled and committed
- [ ] Budget approved and available
- [ ] Tools and infrastructure ready
- [ ] Data access confirmed
- [ ] Stakeholders aligned and informed
- [ ] Sprint calendar created with all key dates
- [ ] Documentation space set up
- [ ] Communication channels established

When every box is checked, you're ready to begin.

## Chapter 2: Selecting Your Sprint Target

Not every AI opportunity is right for a 90-day sprint. Choose a target that's too ambitious, and you'll end day 90 with nothing working. Choose one that's too trivial, and you'll waste the momentum and focus a sprint provides.

This chapter gives you a framework for selecting the perfect sprint target.

### Criteria for 90-Day Achievability

The ideal sprint target meets all these criteria:

#### 1. Clear Problem Definition

You can articulate exactly what problem you're solving and why it matters. There's no ambiguity about what success looks like.

**Good:** "Our customer service team spends 4 hours per day answering the same 10 questions. We want to automate responses to these common inquiries."

**Poor:** "We want to improve customer service with AI."

#### 2. Available Data

You have (or can quickly get) the data needed to train, test, and operate the AI solution.

**Good:** "We have 5,000 historical customer service conversations with resolutions that we can use to train a chatbot."

**Poor:** "We'll collect data as we go." (This adds months to your timeline.)

#### 3. Defined Scope

The boundaries of the project are clear. You know exactly what's in scope and what's not.

**Good:** "The AI will handle the 10 most common support questions. Complex issues will still route to human agents."

**Poor:** "The AI will handle all customer service." (Too broad for 90 days.)

#### 4. Technical Feasibility

The solution you need either exists as a ready-to-use tool or can be built with reasonable effort.

**Good:** "Several no-code chatbot platforms can handle our requirements."

**Poor:** "We need to build custom AI models from scratch." (Possible, but probably not in 90 days for a small team.)

#### 5. Business Value

Success will deliver meaningful value to your business—whether that's cost savings, revenue growth, or capability expansion.

**Good:** "Automating these inquiries will free up 20 hours per week of agent time, worth approximately $25,000 annually."

**Poor:** "It would be cool to have AI." (Technology for technology's sake rarely succeeds.)

#### 6. Organizational Readiness

Your team and organization are ready to adopt the solution.

**Good:** "The customer service team is excited about AI and eager to participate. Management has approved the project."

**Poor:** "The team is skeptical and leadership is lukewarm." (You can overcome resistance, but it takes time you don't have in a sprint.)

### The Sprint Target Evaluation Matrix

Use this matrix to evaluate potential sprint targets:

| Criteria | Weight | Target A Score | Target B Score | Target C Score |
|----------|--------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Problem Clarity | 15% | | | |
| Data Availability | 20% | | | |
| Scope Definition | 15% | | | |
| Technical Feasibility | 20% | | | |
| Business Value | 20% | | | |
| Org Readiness | 10% | | | |
| **Weighted Total** | 100% | | | |

Score each target 1-5 on each criterion, multiply by the weight, and sum. The highest score indicates your best sprint target.

### Scope Definition

Once you've selected a target, define the scope precisely. Use this framework:

#### In Scope (Must Have)

List the specific features and capabilities that are essential for sprint success. These are non-negotiable—if they're not delivered, the sprint fails.

Example:
- AI chatbot that can answer the 10 most common questions
- Integration with existing helpdesk system
- Escalation to human agents when AI confidence is low
- Basic analytics on conversation volume and resolution

#### Out of Scope (Won't Have)

Be explicit about what's not included. This prevents scope creep and manages expectations.

Example:
- Multi-language support (English only for sprint)
- Voice/phone integration (text chat only)
- Advanced sentiment analysis
- Custom AI model training (use pre-built models)

#### Future Scope (Might Have Later)

Identify capabilities that aren't in this sprint but could be added later. This shows stakeholders you're thinking ahead without overcommitting.

Example:
- Additional languages (Phase 2)
- Voice integration (Phase 2)
- Proactive customer outreach (Phase 3)
- Advanced analytics and insights (Phase 2)

### Success Metrics

Define how you'll measure success. Every sprint needs three types of metrics:

#### 1. Outcome Metrics

These measure business impact. They're what stakeholders care about most.

Examples:
- Response time reduction (hours to minutes)
- Cost savings (dollars per month)
- Revenue increase (percentage or dollars)
- Customer satisfaction change (NPS or CSAT)
- Employee time saved (hours per week)

#### 2. Performance Metrics

These measure how well the AI solution works.

Examples:
- Accuracy rate (percentage of correct responses)
- Confidence scores (AI certainty in its answers)
- Resolution rate (percentage of issues resolved without human intervention)
- Response time (how fast the AI responds)
- Uptime (system availability percentage)

#### 3. Adoption Metrics

These measure whether people are actually using the solution.

Examples:
- Daily active users
- Feature utilization rates
- User satisfaction scores
- Training completion rates
- Support ticket volume (for the AI system itself)

#### Baseline Measurement

Before day 1, measure your baseline for all key metrics. You can't prove improvement if you don't know where you started.

Example baseline report:

```
SPRINT BASELINE METRICS
Sprint: Customer Service AI Chatbot
Date: [Pre-Sprint Date]

OUTCOME METRICS:
- Average response time: 4.2 hours
- Cost per interaction: $12.50
- Customer satisfaction (CSAT): 3.8/5
- Agent hours per week on common questions: 80 hours

PERFORMANCE METRICS:
- N/A (no AI system yet)

ADOPTION METRICS:
- N/A (no AI system yet)
```

| Data quality issues | High | Medium | Data audit before sprint, budget time for cleaning |
| User resistance | Medium | High | Early involvement, comprehensive training, executive support |
| Integration complexity | Medium | High | Technical spike in week 1, engage vendor support early |
| Scope creep | High | Medium | Strict change control, clear scope documentation |
| Performance issues | Medium | Medium | Load testing in month 2, performance monitoring |

### Go/No-Go Decision

Before proceeding to day 1, make a formal go/no-go decision. Ask:

# Part II: Month 1: Foundation (Days 1-30)

The first month of your sprint is about building the foundation. By day 30, you should have a working prototype that demonstrates core functionality. It won't be perfect, it won't be fully integrated, but it will prove that your approach is viable.

## Chapter 3: Week 1-2: Setup and Learning

The first two weeks of your sprint are critical. This is when you set up your tools, train your team, prepare your data, and configure your environment. Get this right, and the rest of your sprint flows smoothly. Rush it, and you'll fight technical debt and confusion for the next 10 weeks.

### Tool Selection and Procurement

By day 1, you should have a shortlist of potential tools. Now it's time to make final decisions and get everything in place.

#### The Tool Selection Process

**Day 1-2: Final Evaluation**

Even if you did research during preparation, spend the first two days of your sprint doing hands-on evaluation:

1. **Sign up for free trials** of your top 2-3 options
2. **Run through tutorials** to understand the basics
3. **Test with your actual data** (a small sample)
4. **Evaluate the user interface**—is it intuitive for your team?
5. **Check integration capabilities**—can it connect to your existing systems?
6. **Assess support quality**—send a test question and see how fast they respond

**Day 3: Decision and Procurement**

Make your tool selection based on:
- Fit with your requirements (must-haves vs. nice-to-haves)
- Ease of use for your team
- Integration capabilities
- Support quality
- Total cost of ownership (not just the subscription price)

Complete procurement on day 3. Don't let purchasing delays slow your sprint.

#### Tool Selection Case Study: Maria's Accounting Firm

Maria needed a tool to automate document processing for her accounting firm. She evaluated three options:

| Criteria | Tool A | Tool B | Tool C |
|----------|--------|--------|--------|
| Accuracy on test docs | 94% | 89% | 96% |
| Setup time | 2 hours | 8 hours | 4 hours |
| Integration with QuickBooks | Native | API available | Third-party only |
| Cost | $299/mo | $149/mo | $199/mo |
| Support response time | 4 hours | 24 hours | 12 hours |

Maria chose Tool A despite the higher cost because the native QuickBooks integration and faster support would save her team time—time that was worth more than the price difference.

#### Essential Tools for Most Sprints

While every sprint is different, most need:

**AI Platform:** The core AI tool (chatbot platform, document processor, prediction service, etc.)

**Project Management:** Tool to track tasks and progress (Trello, Asana, Monday, or even a simple spreadsheet)

**Documentation:** Wiki or shared drive for storing documentation (Notion, Confluence, Google Docs, SharePoint)

**Communication:** Channel for sprint team communication (Slack, Teams, or dedicated email thread)

**Version Control:** If you're building custom solutions, Git repository (GitHub, GitLab, Bitbucket)

**Testing Environment:** Separate from production for safe experimentation

### Team Training

Your team needs to learn the tools and concepts they'll use during the sprint. But don't over-train—focus on what they need to know for the next two weeks.

#### The Just-in-Time Training Approach

Instead of trying to teach everything upfront, train just before knowledge is needed:

**Week 1 Training:**
- Tool basics (day 3-4)
- Data preparation (day 5)
- Environment setup (day 6-7)

**Week 2 Training:**
- Core functionality (day 8-9)
- Integration basics (day 10-11)
- Testing procedures (day 12-14)

This approach keeps training relevant and reduces the forgetting curve.

#### Training Resources

**Vendor Resources:**
- Documentation and tutorials
- Webinars and training videos
- Certification programs
- Community forums

**External Resources:**
- Online courses (Udemy, Coursera, LinkedIn Learning)
- YouTube tutorials
- Industry blogs and publications
- Peer communities and user groups

**Internal Resources:**
- Knowledge sharing sessions
- Documentation of learnings
- Pair programming/learning
- Sprint team workshops

### Data Preparation

AI is only as good as the data it learns from. Week 1-2 includes critical data preparation work.

#### The Data Preparation Process

**Step 1: Data Inventory (Day 4-5)**

Identify all data sources:
- What data do you have?
- Where is it stored?
- What format is it in?
- Who owns it?
- How do you access it?

**Step 2: Data Quality Assessment (Day 6-7)**

Evaluate your data quality:
- Completeness: Are there missing values?
- Accuracy: Is the data correct?
- Consistency: Are formats and values consistent?
- Timeliness: Is the data current?
- Relevance: Does the data relate to your objective?

**Step 3: Data Cleaning (Day 8-10)**

Fix data quality issues:
- Remove duplicates
- Fill or flag missing values
- Standardize formats
- Correct errors

### Parallel Operation

Don't flip a switch and go all-in on day one. Parallel operation lets you validate the AI solution while maintaining a safety net.

#### The Parallel Operation Approach

**Phase 1: Shadow Mode (Week 5)**

The AI runs alongside existing processes but doesn't control anything:
- AI makes predictions or recommendations
- Humans review AI output
- Decisions are made the old way
- Compare AI suggestions to human decisions

This validates AI accuracy without risk.

**Phase 2: Assisted Operation (Week 6)**

The AI handles routine cases, humans handle exceptions:
- AI processes cases it has high confidence in
- Humans review AI decisions (sample or all)
- Humans handle cases AI can't process
- Gradually increase AI autonomy as confidence builds

**Phase 3: Supervised Operation (End of Week 6)**

AI handles most cases with human oversight:
- AI processes majority of cases automatically
- Humans monitor and intervene as needed
- Regular review of AI performance
- Exception handling well-defined

Return on Investment is the ultimate measure of sprint success. Calculate both tangible and intangible returns.

#### Tangible ROI Calculation

**Costs:**

| Cost Category | Amount |
|--------------|--------|
| Software/Tools | $ |
| External Services | $ |
| Internal Labor (hours × rate) | $ |
| Training | $ |
| Infrastructure | $ |
| **Total Investment** | **$** |

**Returns:**

| Return Category | Annual Amount |
|----------------|---------------|
| Labor Cost Savings | $ |
| Revenue Increase | $ |
| Error Reduction Savings | $ |
| Productivity Gains | $ |
| Other Quantifiable Benefits | $ |
| **Total Annual Return** | **$** |

**ROI Calculation:**
```
ROI = (Total Annual Return - Total Investment) / Total Investment × 100%

Payback Period = Total Investment / (Total Annual Return / 12) months
```

#### ROI Case Study: Sarah's Predictive Maintenance

Sarah's AI sprint investment and returns:

**Investment:**
- AI platform: $3,600/year
- Implementation labor: $8,000
- Training: $1,200
- **Total Investment: $12,800**

**Annual Returns:**
- Avoided downtime (3 incidents × $5,000): $15,000
- Reduced maintenance costs: $8,000
- Extended equipment life: $5,000
- **Total Annual Return: $28,000**

**ROI:** (28,000 - 12,800) / 12,800 × 100% = **119%**
**Payback Period:** 5.5 months

## Retrospective Framework

```
SPRINT RETROSPECTIVE FRAMEWORK

PREPARATION (Before Meeting):
□ Schedule within 1 week of sprint completion
□ Invite sprint team and key stakeholders
□ Prepare retrospective board (physical or digital)
□ Send pre-work if desired

MATERIALS NEEDED:
□ Whiteboard or digital equivalent
□ Sticky notes or digital cards
□ Timer
□ Template for documentation

RETROSPECTIVE BOARD COLUMNS:
1. What Went Well (Successes)
2. What Didn't Work (Challenges)
3. Lessons Learned (Insights)
4. Action Items (Improvements)

FACILITATION GUIDE:

Opening (5 min):
- Welcome participants
- Review purpose and ground rules
- Set positive, constructive tone

Individual Reflection (10 min):
- Each person writes sticky notes for:
  - What went well (3 items)
  - What didn't work (3 items)
- One item per sticky note
- No discussion yet

Group Sharing - Successes (15 min):
- Each person shares their "what went well" items
- Group similar items together
- Discuss patterns and themes
- Capture key successes on board

Group Sharing - Challenges (15 min):
- Each person shares their "what didn't work" items
- Group similar items together
- Discuss root causes
- Focus on learning, not blaming

Prioritization (10 min):
- Dot vote on most important challenges to address
- Identify top 3-5 items for action planning

Action Planning (20 min):
- For each priority item:
  - What would we do differently?
  - Who will own the action?
  - When will it be done?
- Write actionable items (specific, assignable, time-bound)

Closing (5 min):
- Summarize key learnings
- Confirm action items and owners
- Thank participants
- Schedule follow-up if needed

GROUND RULES:
1. Blameless - Focus on processes, not people
2. Honest - Share real experiences
3. Constructive - Aim to improve
4. Participatory - Everyone contributes
5. Action-oriented - Every insight leads to action
6. Timeboxed - Stay on schedule

OUTPUTS:
□ Completed retrospective board
□ Documented lessons learned
□ Action item list with owners and dates
□ Updated templates based on learnings
□ Retrospective report shared with stakeholders
```

1 - Negligible (minimal impact on sprint)

## Conclusion

The 90-Day AI Sprint framework gives small businesses a practical, proven approach to implementing AI. By focusing on a single objective, working in focused iterations, and measuring results, you can achieve meaningful AI capabilities without the complexity and cost that derail many AI initiatives.

Remember:
- **Focus beats breadth:** Do one thing exceptionally well
- **Done is better than perfect:** Ship working solutions and iterate
- **People before technology:** Adoption determines success
- **Measure everything:** If you can't measure it, you can't improve it
- **Learn and adapt:** Every sprint makes the next one better

Your AI journey starts with a single sprint. Make it count.
